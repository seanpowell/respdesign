What we're going to cover (and what we're not)

We will cover a few new(er) topics around mobile marketing and mobile development - namely responsive design and mobile specific design. Including along the way key concepts for working within these mobile strategies and how it applies to marketers and marketing students. We'll stop short of going into when to build web applications and other subconcepts to responsive design but will touch on these topics at a high level.

After we're done, we'll be able to answer these questions:

  • Where and how those concepts are being used (show and tell)?
  • Why one strategy is better than another (caveat alert: in specific situations)?
  • How do these strategies work with email (if at all)?

Responsive Design is

Responsive web design is an approach to web development and design where the website adapts to the user environment's screen size, device, and orientation.  This is typically done through a combination of flexible grid patterns, images, and css media queries.

In this approach, the actual site location never changes, e.g. to a mobile specific version, just adjusts in real time as the screen size changes.

A tell tale sign of a responsively implemented web site is if you adjust your browser screen the website adjusts intelligently - keeping balance and aspect through any browser (re)size.

Upside

  • One design that "responds" to different screen sizes. Similar experience for all users.
  • Lower time/cost to implement versus multiple separate builds for mobile, tablet, etc.
  • Keeping multiple devices in mind during development generally results in a clearer, simpler layout.
  • SEO: All links and reputation mesurement goes back to one site which is helps improve results of search engine optimization and marketing.
  • Easier to track through analytics.
  • Content publishing is {slightly} simplified.

Downside

  • One design sometimes just can't be perfect for all scenarios.
  • Displays at the smallest level generally take a performance hit.
  • SEO: Because you are using the same content, it's harder to adjust content/copy for mobile specific searching.

Mobile specific design

Mobile design is building specifically for the small screen. This approach builds a unique experience for mobile (smartphone) users and possibly tablets. The hallmarks of a mobile design are lightweight elements, scant of (heavy or large) imagery, and optimization for simple navigation. Generally speaking, choices are presented to the user in smaller numbers, representing the sometimes very different purpose of mobile traffic. A separate site, and in most cases code base, is created and maintained from the main website or application.

Upside

  • Design that is specific to your audience and your audience needs.
  • Optimized for bandwidth constraints of mobile users.

Downside

  • Generally implemented on a separate domain or at least a separate page therefore diluting page rank and overall seo (to a certain extent).
  • Costs associated with developing a corresponding website to your desktop website.

Note: This is not to be confused with "Mobile First" design which is an approach to your overall site design where the mobile phone leads as your first design and you build up from there.

Email and mobile design

While your flexibility is limited there are still ways to employ responsive like tactics to target mobile devices in email. By understanding a few simple concepts, marketers can tweak their email marketing to function optimally on a mobile phone while keeping the desktop experience unchanged.

"Why optimize your email for mobile? If you send email newsletters, it's likely that a growing percentage of your subscribers are reading your messages on an iPhone, tablet or similar. When we last surveyed email client popularity in 2011, we found that almost 20% of email opens occurred on a mobile device, rising dramatically from a mere 4% of the market when we last ran the numbers in 2009. iOS devices accounted for over 90% of these reported opens." - Campaign Monitor's Resposive Email Design Guide (http://www.campaignmonitor.com/guides/mobile/)

Upside

  • Call to actions and other items are generally better placed.
  • Generally results in a simpler, one column design that works well in email.

Downside

  • More expensive, time wise, to implement.

A quick note about content and strategic considerations

When we talk about how to create products and services for mobile, the conversation tends to focus on design and development challenges. How does our design aesthetic change when we’re dealing with a smaller (or higher-resolution) screen? How do we employ (and teach) new gestural interactions that take advantage of touchscreen capabilities? How (and who) will write the code for all these different platforms—and how will we maintain all of them?

Great questions, every one. But focusing just on the design and development questions leaves out one important subject: how are we going to get our content to render appropriately on mobile devices? - Content Strategy for Mobile by Karen McGrane
